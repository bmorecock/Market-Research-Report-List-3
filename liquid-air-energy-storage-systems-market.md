<p><p>-Liquid Air Energy Storage Systems: Also known as cryogenic energy storage, these systems store energy in the form of liquid air or liquefied gases at low temperatures. When energy is needed, the liquid air is warmed to expand and drive a turbine to generate electricity.</p><p>-Solid State Batteries: These batteries use solid electrolytes instead of liquid or gel electrolytes, providing improved safety, energy density, and lifespan.</p><p>-Flow Batteries: These batteries store energy in liquid electrolytes contained in separate tanks, allowing for scalable energy storage capacity.</p><p>-Flywheels: These systems store energy by spinning a rotor at high speeds, releasing the energy when needed by slowing down the rotor.</p><p>-Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES): This system compresses air into storage, releasing it to generate electricity when needed.</p><p>-Thermal Energy Storage: This method stores energy in the form of heat or cold, which can be used to generate electricity or provide heating and cooling.</p><p>-Pumped Hydro-Power: This system stores energy by pumping water to a higher reservoir during times of excess electricity generation, releasing it through turbines to generate electricity when needed.</p></p>
